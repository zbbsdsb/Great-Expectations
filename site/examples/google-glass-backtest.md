# Example — Back-test: Google Glass (2013–2015)

> Input: "Why did Google Glass fail, and what would Great Expectations have flagged before launch?"
> Consensus: Wearable AR will diffuse like smartphones; tech enthusiasts preview the mass market.
> Scarcest resource: social license (public trust about being recorded).

## 1. Assumption Inventory
- `CONSENSUS` — "A breakthrough device sells on specs to early adopters, then crosses to the mainstream." (False for socially-mediated wearables.)
- `CONSENSUS` — "If enthusiasts love it, the market will follow." (Confused vocal power-users with the silent majority.)
- `SYSTEM-1` — "Camera-on-face is just a better hands-free phone." (Ignored the *other-person* threat perception.)
- `ATOMIC` — Glasses = (FOV display) + (forward camera) + (social acceptability) + (power/weight budget).

## 2. First-Principles Decomposition
AR glasses are not "a phone on your face." They are a **bidirectional surveillance object**: they output to you AND capture others. The binding constraint is not battery or pixel density — it is the **consent of bystanders**, a non-engineering variable.

## 3. Disruptive Pathways
- **Path A — "Consent by default."** Replace the silent camera with an always-visible recording indicator + a local LED ring bright enough to be unmistakable at 3m. Breaks the value-realization assumption that capture can be covert.
- **Path B — "Enterprise-only beachhead."** Abandon consumer launch; sell to surgeons/warehouse pickers where being recorded is contractual, not a social threat. Breaks the "mass-market first" growth assumption.

## 4. Completeness Mapping
- **① Stakeholder hologram:** The *bystander* — a role the plan never named — acquired a new power: public shaming ("Glasshole"). Finance/PR roles gained a crisis KPI overnight. **Missing in original launch.**
- **② Resource antifragility:** Remove "social license" (one of four resource classes) and the plan is dead. The original design had **zero** substitute for it. **FAIL — resource fragility point.**
- **③ 2nd / 3rd order:** 1st: enthusiasts adopt. 2nd: venues (bars, casinos) ban the device. 3rd: a social narrative ("creepy") hardens into local ordinances banning head-cam wearables. **Original plan stopped at 1st order.**
- **④ Spatiotemporal redundancy:** The most irreversible node was the *first* public impression. Once "Glasshole" anchored, no Plan B could reset the story. **No strategic options were pre-bought.**

## 5. Completeness Stress-Test Report
| Test | Verdict |
|------|---------|
| Stakeholder hologram | FAIL — bystander role absent |
| Resource antifragility | FAIL — social license had no substitute |
| 2nd/3rd-order | FAIL — stopped at 1st order |
| Spatiotemporal redundancy | FAIL — no Plan B at the irreversible node |

**Paths A and B both pass where the original failed.** The original is **voided**.

## 6. Vulnerability Stress Test (BANI)
Under *Anxious + Incomprehensible*: a single viral "creepshot" clip (Non-linear shock) collapses the entire consumer thesis in 72 hours. Kill-switch: a hard, non-bypassable consent UI + immediate enterprise pivot.

## 7. Cognitive Bias Audit
- **Overconfidence** — assumed engineering excellence substituted for social adoption.
- **Tech-centric bias** — optimized the wearer, ignored the witnessed.
- **Availability** — over-weighted enthusiastic testers.

## 8. Falsifiable Assertions
1. If a visible, unmistakable recording indicator had shipped at launch, bystander-ban incidents would have fallen by >50% in the first 6 months.
2. An enterprise-first launch would have reached >$100M revenue before any consumer attempt.
3. Without a consent UI, no amount of hardware iteration changes the adoption curve.
